# Niyora — "Understand" content cards (v1)

The reframe content for the **Understand** shelf. Read cards: tap from the shelf, the passage holds on screen.

**Voice:** neutrally warm, like a smart Californian friend who happens to know the science. Casual, contractions, affirmative (say what IS true, avoid "not... not..." negation chains). No em dashes. No instructional promises ("this will help"). Never "it's fine / it's normal." See [[project-content-voice]].

**Provenance:** every claim below survived a 3-skeptic adversarial verification pass (deep-research, 2026-06-18). Refuted claims were cut: the "premenstrual animosity targets your partner / marriages deteriorate" angle (refuted 0-3), the acetylcholine brain-fog mechanism (unsupported), premenstrual inflammation as a driver (weak/mixed, CRP & TNF-alpha rises refuted), and "cravings are serotonin-seeking" (unverified). ## Core cards (eligible across every feeling)

### Your hormones are normal

Okay, here's the part nobody tells you. Your hormones are completely normal this week. Totally regular levels.
When researchers measured them, women who feel awful before their period had the exact same levels as women who feel great.
The whole difference is in how strongly your brain responds to the normal ups and downs.
So this is your body being extra tuned in to the shift. Doing a lot with a normal amount.

*Source: NIMH / Schmidt & Rubinow differential-sensitivity model; Hantsoo & Payne 2020 — https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2352289520300035*

### Why your brain feels it more

This sensitivity is built in, and a lot of it runs in families. Premenstrual sensitivity is about 56 percent heritable.
Researchers looked at the actual cells of women who struggle before their period, and found they respond differently to the very same hormones.
So this comes from how you're wired. It's a real, physical trait, kind of like having sensitive skin.
The same shift other people barely clock, your system actually feels.

*Source: Dubey et al. 2017, Molecular Psychiatry (ESC/E(Z) gene complex) — https://www.nature.com/articles/mp2016229; NIMH — https://www.nih.gov/news-events/news-releases/sex-hormone-sensitive-gene-complex-linked-premenstrual-mood-disorder*

### This has an end date

Here's something solid to hold onto. When researchers switched these hormones off, the symptoms cleared. When they switched them back on, the symptoms came back.
That's the clearest proof that what you feel is tied to a passing hormonal moment, and your body moves through it.
It shows up with the shift, and it eases with the next one. Every single cycle.
Think of it as weather. It rolls in, and it rolls out.

*Source: NIMH GnRH-agonist add-back studies — https://www.nih.gov/news-events/news-releases/sex-hormone-sensitive-gene-complex-linked-premenstrual-mood-disorder*

### This is older than you

Turns out this runs way beyond humans. The exact same hormone change creates the exact same effect in other mammals. Scientists study it in rats.
And it lands harder on more sensitive nervous systems, same shift, bigger response.
So what you feel before your period is a biological pattern way older than you.
You inherited this. It's biology doing its old, familiar thing.

*Source: progesterone-withdrawal rodent model; Hantsoo & Payne 2020 — https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2352289520300035; https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC9266311/*

### You were right all along

If you've ever felt brushed off, the history is on your side.
This was only recognized as a real medical condition in 2013. Before that, women waited years, often more than a decade, and saw a string of doctors before anyone put a name to it.
For most of medical history, women's cyclic struggles got waved away or brushed off as just emotional.
So if no one explained this to you, that's the system catching up slowly. You were reading your own body right.

*Source: Dell'Osso et al. 2024, Frontiers in Psychiatry — https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/psychiatry/articles/10.3389/fpsyt.2024.1458114/full; IAPMD diagnostic timeline — https://www.iapmd.org/diagnostic-recognition-timeline*

## By feeling

### Anxious — Why everything feels like a threat

Your body has a built-in calm, a kind of brake on fear that usually works quietly in the background.
In the days before your period, that brake responds differently, and it eases off.
So the same text, the same silence, the same small thing reads as a threat.
Your alarm is just turned up extra sensitive today, and it settles as the shift passes.

*Source: allopregnanolone / GABA-A sensitivity; Hantsoo & Payne 2020 — https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2352289520300035*

### Irritable — Why the smallest things set you off

That same calm that usually absorbs little frustrations is running thin this week.
So things you'd brush off any other day feel genuinely unbearable right now.
The anger is real, and it's turned all the way up this week. That's the dial, and you're still you underneath it.
It softens as your body moves past the shift.

*Source: allopregnanolone / GABA-A sensitivity; premenstrual prefrontal serotonin shift correlates with irritability (preliminary, Eriksson 2016) — https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC5019404/*

### Low / tearful — Why you can't feel happy right now

The part of you that reaches for joy is turned way down right now, like someone lowered the dial.
So forcing yourself to feel happy is like flooring a car in neutral. The engine's fine, it just needs the dial turned back up, and that happens on its own.
You're whole, and this passes.
The heaviness lifts when your hormones move on. That relief you feel after is your body coming back online.

*Based on the differential-sensitivity model (a plain-language description of the brain's dampened response to the normal hormonal shift, not a specific study result). Model: Schmidt & Rubinow / Hantsoo & Payne 2020 — https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2352289520300035*

### Foggy — Why your head feels foggy

Good news first, because you deserve it. Tested and proven, your memory and focus stay just as sharp before your period. Your brain keeps running at full strength.
What actually shifts is where your attention goes. It keeps drifting toward the heavy stuff, the self-critical, the worst-case.
So the fog is real, and it's really about your focus getting pulled somewhere darker for a few days.
Your mind is fully here the whole time.

*Source: 2025 meta-analysis, no robust cyclic cognitive change — https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371%2Fjournal.pone.0318576; negative attentional bias central to PMDD (Henderson 2025) — https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0165032724018676*

### Overwhelmed — Why everything feels like too much

Same plate, smaller hands. Your capacity quietly shrank this week.
The system that helps you absorb stress is running at lower power right now, so a regular load feels impossible.
Same week, next cycle, this'll feel totally doable again.
Your buffer's just thin at the moment, and it refills on its own.

*Based on the differential-sensitivity model (reduced capacity to buffer stress is a description of that model, not a measured finding). Model: Schmidt & Rubinow / Hantsoo & Payne 2020 — https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2352289520300035*

## General-context cards (`context: general`)

The two-level model: the Understand shelf is a library of atomic cards tagged by **feeling** + **context** (`general` | `pms`). Default shows the `general` card; a "before my period" chip (or a PMS signal) surfaces the matching `pms` card. No card gets rewritten, we just add these general ones alongside. Same voice, pure reframe (activity cards handle "what to do"). Universal material is well-sourced from the research (neuroception, felt safety, the calming-system science).

### Anxious — Why everything feels like a threat
Your body has an alarm system, and right now it's turned up high.
When you're worn down, the part of your brain that watches for danger starts reading ordinary things, a text, a silence, a small task, as threats.
The feeling is real, and it's also louder than the actual situation.
Your alarm is just running hot, and it settles.

### Irritable — Why the smallest things set you off
Irritability usually means one thing: your reserves are low.
When you're depleted, the small stuff you'd normally shrug off lands sharp instead.
The anger is real, and it's running on empty.
It's the low reserves talking, and they refill.

### Low / tearful — Why you feel heavy
Low moods are a state your brain moves through, not a personal failing.
When you're tired or stretched, the part of you that reaches for joy goes quiet, so forcing happiness rarely lands.
You don't have to fix it or explain it.
You're whole, and the heaviness passes.

### Foggy — Why your head feels foggy
Brain fog feels like thinking through cotton, and your mind is still working underneath it.
When you're tired or overloaded, focus gets harder to hold and your attention drifts to the heavy stuff.
You're not losing your edge.
The sharpness comes back once you've rested.

### Overwhelmed — Why everything feels like too much
Overwhelm is usually a capacity thing, not a you thing.
When your buffer is thin, an ordinary load feels impossible. Same plate, smaller hands.
Your capacity is just low right now, and it refills.

### Feeling safe (universal)
Here's something your body does on its own: it decides whether you're safe before your mind even catches up.
So calm isn't really a thought, it's a state. Slower breath, warmth, a steady presence, and your body starts to believe the danger has passed.
You can't always think your way calm.
But your body can lead, and the mind follows.

*Source (universal): neuroception / felt safety has a measurable autonomic correlate — https://academic.oup.com/abm/article/59/1/kaaf014/8102088; Porges felt-safety substrate.*

## Verified facts bank (for other copy: numbers, hooks)

- ~80% of women feel premenstrual symptoms; ~5-8% severely. Near-universal. (https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/eva.12190)
- PMS is ~56% heritable. (NIMH)
- PMDD became a full DSM diagnosis only in 2013 (DSM-IV appendix 1994; ICD-11 added 2019, effective 2022).
- Historic diagnostic delay ~12-20 years; some saw up to 10 providers; down to ~2 years for newly recognized cases since 2013.
- Your body appraises "safe" before conscious thought (neuroception); felt safety has a measurable autonomic correlate and tracks higher vagal HRV. (https://academic.oup.com/abm/article/59/1/kaaf014/8102088)
- Evolutionary "why" = competing hypotheses only (label as "one idea," never fact).
